## SEQUOIA FRAMEWORK

### Module 1 — Category Definition
*"Is this company defining and owning a category, or competing in one?"*

Sequoia's most valued investments are category creators or category leaders
with first-mover advantages that compound over time.

For {{SYMBOL}} in {{SECTOR}}:
- What category does this company operate in, and what is its market share?
- Is this category growing, stable, or declining in India?
- Is {{SYMBOL}} the category leader, challenger, or follower?
- Could a well-capitalised entrant (large Indian conglomerate, foreign
  multinational, well-funded startup) replicate this position in 5 years?

Answer with specifics. "It has a strong brand" is not enough — explain
what specifically makes the brand a moat in this category.

### Module 2 — Moat Audit (5 Sources)
Rate each moat source 1–5 (1 = none, 5 = exceptional).
Provide one paragraph of evidence for each non-zero rating.

**2a. Network Effects** (Score: ?)
Does the product become more valuable as more users/customers join?
- Examples in Indian context: payment networks (PhonePe, NPCI),
  B2B marketplaces, logistics aggregators
- For most traditional businesses this is 1 — be honest
- Do not conflate "market share" with "network effects"

**2b. Switching Costs** (Score: ?)
How painful is it for a customer to leave?
- High switching cost examples: ERP software, core banking, industrial
  process equipment with proprietary consumables, long-term contracts
- For FMCG: switching cost is near zero — brand is the only moat
- Revenue CAGR of {{REVENUE_GROWTH_3YR}}% vs churn context

**2c. Cost Advantage** (Score: ?)
Can this company produce cheaper than any realistic competitor?
- Scale advantages (largest player gets raw material or logistics pricing)
- Proprietary process / technology
- Captive distribution that reduces cost-to-serve
- Operating margin of {{OPERATING_MARGIN}}% — compare vs sector average

**2d. Intangible Assets** (Score: ?)
Brands, patents, licenses, data assets:
- Brand: Does brand command price premium? By how much?
- Regulatory licenses: Banking license, insurance license, spectrum —
  these create near-impenetrable moats in Indian regulated industries
- Data: Does proprietary data create compounding advantage (e.g., credit
  bureaus, insurance data, supply chain data)?

**2e. Efficient Scale** (Score: ?)
Is the market size such that only one or two players can operate
profitably? Dominant player in a niche is protected by economics:
- Regional leaders in infrastructure, utilities, toll roads
- Specialised B2B suppliers in narrow industrial categories
- Port operators, airport operators with geographic monopoly

**Moat Aggregate Score**: Sum of 5 above / 25 = ___/5
**Moat Direction**: Widening / Stable / Narrowing
(Most important: a 3/5 moat that is widening is better than a 4/5 that is eroding)

### Module 3 — Unit Economics Quality
Sequoia demands that the business makes money at the unit level before
it deserves growth capital.

For {{SYMBOL}} with operating margin of {{OPERATING_MARGIN}}% and ROE {{ROE}}%:

**3a. Margin Structure**
- Is operating margin expanding or contracting over 3 years?
- If revenue grows but margins compress: pricing power is weakening
- {{REVENUE_GROWTH_3YR}}% revenue growth with {{PAT_GROWTH_3YR}}% PAT growth:
  Is operating leverage working (PAT growing faster than revenue)?

**3b. Return on Incremental Capital (ROIC)**
- Is ROE of {{ROE}}% above or below industry cost of capital (~13–14% in India)?
- Is the company generating value (ROE > 14%) or destroying it (ROE < 14%)?
- D/E of {{DEBT_EQUITY}}: Is growth being funded by retained earnings (high quality)
  or by taking on debt (lower quality)?

**3c. Capital Intensity**
- High capex / revenue ratio: Capital-intensive businesses require constant
  reinvestment and limit free cash flow
- Asset-light businesses (software, financial services, platforms):
  These scale with minimal incremental capital — Sequoia's preference

### Module 4 — Scalability Assessment
*"Can this business 10x revenue without a corresponding 10x in costs?"*

For {{SYMBOL}} in the Indian context:
- What are the primary growth levers? (Geographic expansion, new products,
  category penetration, premiumisation, export)
- What breaks at scale? (Key person dependency, supply chain constraints,
  regulatory headwinds, quality control at volume)
- Pan-India addressability: India has 28 states with varying regulatory
  environments — how difficult is national scale for this business model?

### Module 5 — Retention & Customer Quality
- B2B or B2C? B2B typically has higher switching costs and LTV
- Revenue concentration: Top 10 customers as % of revenue
  (>40% in one customer is a risk; it is also a switching cost signal)
- Repeat purchase / recurring revenue characteristics
- Revenue CAGR {{REVENUE_GROWTH_3YR}}%: Is this volume-led or
  price-led growth? Price-led = stronger moat evidence.

## SEQUOIA VERDICT

### Business Quality Rating
- **Exceptional (A)**: Moat score ≥4/5, expanding margins, ROIC >20%, category leader.
  These are the buy-and-never-sell businesses.
- **Good (B)**: Moat score 3/5, stable-to-expanding margins, ROIC >14%.
  Worth owning at fair valuations.
- **Average (C)**: Moat score 2/5, commodity-like pricing, thin margins.
  Only at deep value prices.
- **Avoid (D)**: No discernible moat, declining margins, capital-intensive
  with sub-cost-of-capital returns.
